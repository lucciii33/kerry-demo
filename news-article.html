Caribbean Cultural Identity at Work:
A Hidden Leadership Advantage

Jun 24, 2025 Carry On Friends Dr. Kerry Mitchell Brown 8 min read

For Caribbean American professionals navigating corporate America, cultural identity has long been treated as something to soften, translate, or leave at the door. Dr. Kerry Mitchell Brown argues the opposite — that this identity is a strategic leadership asset hiding in plain sight.

In a recent conversation on Carry On Friends, Dr. Brown unpacked the ways Caribbean upbringing cultivates a distinct leadership orientation — one defined by collectivism, resilience, code-switching mastery, and a long-horizon view of success that many organizations desperately need but rarely know how to name.

"We grew up watching our parents navigate systems that were not designed for them," she explained. "That teaches you something about strategic patience, about reading rooms, about understanding power — not as something handed to you, but as something you build."

The qualities corporations say they want — adaptability, cultural intelligence, community orientation — are exactly what Caribbean American women have been developing their entire lives. The question is whether organizations are willing to recognize it as expertise.

— Dr. Kerry Mitchell Brown

The Code-Switch as Competitive Intelligence

One of the most compelling threads in the conversation was the reframing of code-switching — a survival skill often discussed as a burden — as a form of competitive intelligence. Caribbean professionals, Dr. Brown noted, grow up navigating multiple cultural registers simultaneously: the household, the island diaspora, American professional culture, and often the specific micro-cultures of their industries.

This is not a deficit. It is, she argued, a form of organizational agility that translates directly into effective leadership in complex, cross-cultural environments. The ability to read a room, shift register, and communicate across difference is not something that can be trained in a weekend workshop. It is built over a lifetime.

What Organizations Miss When They Miss This

Dr. Brown was clear that the failure is rarely one of individual ambition. Caribbean American women enter organizations with credentials, drive, and an unusually sophisticated understanding of navigating systems. The failure, she said, is structural — companies that mistake cultural difference for a lack of fit, rather than an untapped leadership signal.

"When an organization says someone isn't 'executive presence' material, it's worth asking: whose presence are we measuring against? Because presence isn't neutral. It was defined by a very specific kind of person in a very specific moment in history."

The strategic implication is direct: organizations that learn to recognize and cultivate Caribbean American leadership talent gain access to professionals who have been training in adaptive intelligence their entire lives. Those that don't continue to lose them to companies that will.

Building the Future On Purpose

The conversation closed on a call to intentionality — both personal and institutional. For Caribbean American professionals, Dr. Brown offered a reframe: stop auditing your cultural identity for palatability. Start treating it as the strategic advantage it actually is.

For organizations, the charge is harder. It requires examining the unconscious definitions embedded in talent systems — what counts as leadership potential, what reads as executive presence, who gets access to sponsorship. The answer, Dr. Brown suggested, will not come from a diversity statement. It will come from structural change at the level of how organizations define and recognize excellence.
